# Transfer, Analogical Reasoning & Argument Schemas — Research & Feature Proposals

_Researcher C · Round-3 memo · to be attacked in debate_

**Persona.** I study **transfer of learning and schema induction**. My thesis for the LSAT is narrow and testable: Logical Reasoning is fundamentally a **structural-recognition** task — the same abstract argument forms and flaw types (correlation→causation, sufficient/necessary confusion, unrepresentative sampling, equivocation, ad hominem, part↔whole) recur under endlessly varied surface topics. Novices encode LR items by **surface topic** ("that was the vaccine one"); experts encode by **deep structure** ("that's a causal-overgeneralization from a biased sample"). The literature says you can *move* a learner from surface to structural representation by making them **compare two structurally-identical, surface-different examples** and abstract the shared schema. No current LSAT tool — and neither of our two sibling memos — trains cross-**surface** schema abstraction or **measures** whether a student has a transferable schema versus a memorized item. That gap is the opportunity.

**Where this sits relative to what we've built and to sibling memos (read this first — it is my whole differentiation).**

- Our **LR type-interleaving** (`lsat/interleaving.py`) only changes study *order* (round-robin by question **type**). It never juxtaposes two items for comparison, never varies surface topic deliberately, and never asks the learner to produce the shared structure. Order ≠ comparison.
- Our **paraphrase eval** (`eval/paraphrase.py`) rewrites **the same item** and measures a recognition→application drop. That is *within-item* near transfer. My proposals go *across items*, holding **deep structure** constant while **maximizing surface difference** — a structural transfer measure, not a rewording one.
- Sibling memo **R2 (error-trap)** proposes "contrasting **trap**-pairs": a *correct* answer vs an *engineered-wrong* answer on a **near-identical stem**, isolating **one distractor axis** (scope/strength/direction). That is **discrimination** training (minimize surface variation to spotlight one difference). Mine is the **opposite manipulation for the opposite goal**: two **independent** arguments with **maximally different** topics that share **one abstract structure**, to force **abstraction** (schema induction). Gick & Holyoak's effect is specifically about *semantically disparate* analogs, so cross-surface comparison — not near-identical trap pairs — is the faithful instantiation of the schema-induction literature.
- Sibling memo **R1 (perceptual learning)** proposes fast structural *classification* ("name the type/flaw in <3 s") and *minimal-pair* discrimination. That trains **speed of recognition** and **fine discrimination**. Mine trains the **representation itself** (does the learner group by structure at all?) and its **transfer across topic**, and measures it. Complementary, not overlapping: R1 makes an existing schema *faster*; I try to *build* the schema and *prove* it transferred.

## Learning-theory foundations

Every empirical bullet: **claim · citation · specific finding · evidence strength.** All URLs were opened during this pass (2026‑07‑01); where a number comes from a secondary source I say so.

- **Comparing two examples induces a transferable schema; one example (even with a stated principle or diagram) does not.** Gick, M. L., & Holyoak, K. J. (1983). "Schema Induction and Analogical Transfer." *Cognitive Psychology, 15*(1), 1–38. https://doi.org/10.1016/0010-0285(83)90002-6 · open PDF: https://deepblue.lib.umich.edu/bitstream/handle/2027.42/25331/0000776.pdf
  *Finding (read in full text):* In Part I, fostering a schema from a **single** analog via summarization, a **verbal statement of the principle**, or a **diagram** "**achieved [no] notable degree of success**." In Part II, giving **two** analogs and asking subjects to **describe their similarities** produced a schema "as an incidental product," and "**the quality of the induced schema was highly predictive of subsequent transfer**." Crucially, the verbal statements and diagrams that failed with one analog "**proved highly beneficial when paired with two**." Transfer on the classic radiation problem rose from ~**10%** (no analog) to ~**30%** (one analog, no hint) to ~**75%** (with hint) — and higher with two analogs. **Evidence: STRONG** (foundational, heavily replicated).

- **Comparing two cases ≈ triples transfer versus studying the same two cases separately; and just *telling* the principle is not enough.** Loewenstein, J., Thompson, L., & Gentner, D. (1999). "Analogical encoding facilitates knowledge transfer in negotiation." *Psychonomic Bulletin & Review, 6*(4), 586–597. https://doi.org/10.3758/BF03212967 · Gentner, D., Loewenstein, J., & Thompson, L. (2003). "Learning and transfer: A general role for analogical encoding." *J. Educational Psychology, 95*(2), 393–408. https://groups.psych.northwestern.edu/gentner/papers/GentnerLoewensteinThompson03.pdf
  *Finding (read in full text):* Management students who **compared** two negotiation cases were "**nearly three times more** likely" to transfer the strategy to a new case than those given the **same cases separately** (Loewenstein et al., 1999, Exp. 2). In the 2003 replication+extension, **47% (15/32)** of guided-comparison learners transferred the trained principle to a surface-different test case vs **6% (1/16)** of a no-comparison baseline (χ²=7.92, p<.01), gains were **specific to the compared principle**, "**the better the schema, the better the transfer**," and **more comparison support → more transfer** (Exp. 3, live negotiation). The companion paper (Gentner, Loewenstein & Thompson, 2004, https://groups.psych.northwestern.edu/gentner/papers/GentnerLoewensteinThompson04.pdf, abstract retrieved) reports that **providing the common principle alone is *not sufficient* — comparison is still beneficial** (and even aids *backward* retrieval of prior cases). **Evidence: STRONG** (multiple experiments, replicated across labs/domains).

- **Analyzing contrasting cases *before* being told creates "a time for telling."** Schwartz, D. L., & Bransford, J. D. (1998). "A Time for Telling." *Cognition and Instruction, 16*(4), 475–522. https://aaalab.stanford.edu/papers/time_for_telling.pdf
  *Finding (read in full text):* Across three experiments, students who **analyzed contrasting cases** (e.g., graphed patterns that differentiate phenomena) and *then* heard a lecture made **more accurate predictions on a novel transfer problem one week later** than controls who (a) read the case features + lecture, (b) **summarized a text + heard the same lecture**, or (c) analyzed the cases **twice with no lecture**. i.e., **cases-alone is insufficient *and* telling-alone is insufficient — you need differentiation *then* telling.** A 2024 review by Schwartz reports the graphing-then-lecture group "**nearly tripled**" transfer vs summary-then-same-lecture (https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/00461520.2024.2397389, secondary). **Evidence: STRONG** (replicated; the "prepare-for-future-learning" line is large).

- **Experts categorize problems by deep structure; novices by surface features — and this shift is learnable.** Chi, M. T. H., Feltovich, P. J., & Glaser, R. (1981). "Categorization and Representation of Physics Problems by Experts and Novices." *Cognitive Science, 5*(2), 121–152. https://doi.org/10.1207/s15516709cog0502_2 · open PDF: http://matt.colorado.edu/teaching/highcog/readings/cfg81.pdf
  *Finding (retrieved):* In sorting tasks, **experts sort physics problems by the underlying principle** ("a kind of 'deep structure'," e.g., conservation of energy) **while novices sort by surface structure** (inclined plane, spring, pulley). Critically, "**with learning, advanced novices begin to categorize problems by the principles with gradual release from dependence on the physical characteristics.**" **Evidence: STRONG** (foundational; the surface→structure shift is one of the most robust results in expertise research). *This makes a sorting task a direct, quantitative read-out of schema/expertise — the basis of Feature 2.*

- **Prompting self-explanation reliably improves learning and transfer (and is a good fit for AI-generated prompts).** Bisra, K., Liu, Q., Nesbit, J. C., Salimi, F., & Winne, P. H. (2018). "Inducing Self-Explanation: A Meta-Analysis." *Educational Psychology Review, 30*(3), 703–725. https://doi.org/10.1007/s10648-018-9434-x · PDF: https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/spaced-repetition/2018-bisra.pdf
  *Finding (retrieved; PDF saved):* Random-effects meta-analysis, **69 effect sizes, 5,917 participants: g = 0.55, 95% CI [0.45, 0.65]**, robust across tasks/subjects/levels. The authors explicitly recommend **computer-generation of self-explanation prompts** — which our AI pipeline already does for cited cards. **Evidence: STRONG** (meta-analysis). *This warrants the "articulate the shared skeleton" step in Feature 1 and the "explain the cluster" step in Feature 2.*

- **CAUTION — far transfer is hard and must be measured along explicit dimensions, not asserted.** Barnett, S. M., & Ceci, S. J. (2002). "When and where do we apply what we learn? A taxonomy for far transfer." *Psychological Bulletin, 128*(4), 612–637. https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-2909.128.4.612 · PDF: https://rapunselshair.pbworks.com/f/barnett_2002.pdf
  *Finding (retrieved; PDF saved):* Transfer varies along **9 dimensions** (content: skill specificity, performance change, memory demands; context: knowledge domain, physical, temporal, functional, social, modality). "**Estimation of a single effect size for far transfer is misguided**"; evidence for transfer is substantial *under some conditions* but many conditions are untested. **Evidence: STRONG (review).** *Debate-relevant nuance: LSAT cross-**surface-topic** transfer is **NEAR** on almost every Barnett–Ceci dimension — same knowledge domain (LR), same task format (5-choice MCQ), same modality/temporal context — differing essentially **only in surface content**. So I am **not** promising notoriously-hard "far transfer"; I am targeting a realistic near-transfer gradient and **measuring the distance-graded curve** rather than claiming a binary win (Feature 3).*

**Design corollaries I will hold myself to (each is falsifiable):**
1. Two surface-different instances beat one instance + a stated rule (Gick & Holyoak) → **twins, not annotated singles.**
2. Comparison must be **active** (produce the schema), because passive exposure and even a supplied principle underperform (Gentner 2004; Gick & Holyoak Part I) → **force a "fill/predict the shared skeleton" step.**
3. **Structure-vs-surface sorting** is a valid expertise read-out (Chi 1981) → use it as both **metric and drill.**
4. Transfer is **graded by distance** and easy to fake with memorization (Barnett & Ceci) → **measure accuracy as a function of surface distance**, never a single number.

Verbatim: "the LSAT will consist of **two scored Logical Reasoning (LR) sections, one scored Reading Comprehension (RC) section, plus one unscored section** of either LR or RC." LSAC replaced Logic Games with a second LR section after a 2019 accessibility settlement; substituting it "had **virtually no impact on overall scoring — analysis of over 200,000 test sessions found the mean score changed by 1/100th of a point**" (N=218,243), and predictive validity for first-year grades "changed by less than 1/100th of a point." Corroborated by LSAC's Specifications page (https://www.lsac.org/lsat/register-lsat/accommodations/specifications-lsat-and-lsat-argumentative-writing) and Reuters (https://www.reuters.com/legal/legalindustry/law-school-admission-test-drop-logic-games-questions-exam-2023-10-18/). **Consequence: Logical Reasoning is now ~2/3 of the scored exam** (our `lsat-taxonomy.yaml` already encodes `lr.exam_weight: 0.66`, `format_as_of: "2024-08"`). **Any LR-transfer feature now touches ~two-thirds of the score.**

- **The highest-skill LR question types are *literally* structure-matching, and the test-writers use surface topic as the distractor.** Parallel Reasoning, Parallel Flaw, and Method-of-Reasoning ask you to match the **abstract form** of an argument regardless of subject matter. From prep sources (market grounding, not peer-reviewed): "**If you replace all content terms with variables (A, B, C), the logical form of the error in the stimulus and the correct answer is identical … structural isomorphism**" and "**topical similarity is usually a distractor**" (Kaplan https://www.kaptest.com/study/lsat/lsat-logical-reasoning-parallel-parallel-flaws/; test-ninjas https://test-ninjas.com/lsat-lr-match-the-flaw; LSAT Demon https://lsatdemon.com/resources/logical-reasoning/parallel-questions). **Flaw questions are the single most common LR type (~7–9 per test)** and require naming an abstract error (LSAThacks https://lsathacks.com/logical-reasoning-question-types/). Our taxonomy already carries `lr.parallel_reasoning`, `lr.parallel_flaw`, `lr.method_of_reasoning`, an `lr.flaw` type with a **15-entry `flaw_catalog`**, `skill.argument_parts` (conclusion vs. evidence), and even `rc.application_analogy` ("most analogous"). **The abstract-structure scaffolding I need already exists as tags — nobody has built a trainer or a measure on top of it.**

**Why this lens is *especially* right for the post-2024 test:** with Logic Games removed, the diagrammable, self-contained puzzle is gone; what remains is **argument analysis at scale** (2 LR + heavy RC argument questions). Success is now dominated by *recognizing the same argument structure beneath a new topic under time* — the exact competence the schema-induction literature knows how to build.

## Proposed novel features

Shared new substrate (small, mostly reuses existing machinery): a first-class **argument-structure schema id**. We already tag items with node ids that ride Anki's tag/sync system (`lsat/taxonomy.py`, `lsat/events.py`); I add a `struct.*` namespace (e.g., `struct.causal_overgeneralization`, `struct.necessary_sufficient_swap`, `struct.unrep_sample_to_general`) — an **abstract argument form** that groups instances **across surface topics**, analogous to how `flaw_catalog` already groups under `lr.flaw`. No schema migration for tagging (it's just more node ids/tags). Features 1–2 are interventions; Feature 3 is the measurement that keeps 1–2 honest.

### Feature: Structure Twins (cross-surface analogical-comparison cards)

- **What it is (learner POV).** A card shows **two arguments side by side with deliberately different topics** (say, a wildlife-conservation argument and a corporate-earnings argument) that share **one abstract structure**. Before any label is revealed, you do a short **active comparison**: (1) *predict/invent* — "these two are the same underneath; how?"; (2) **fill the shared skeleton** — a minimal template (`Premise pattern → [inferential move] → Conclusion`, plus "the move is (un)warranted because …", or the flaw name). Only **after** you commit your answer does the app "tell": it names the schema (`struct.*` / `flaw.*`), shows the filled skeleton, and highlights the **verbatim source span** in each stimulus that anchors the shared move. Later, the same schema returns with a **third, new-topic** twin so you re-abstract rather than recall a pair.

- **Why NOVEL vs generic Anki AND vs our built features.** Generic Anki is single-prompt Q→A recall; there is no two-instance comparison or learner-produced schema. Vs our **interleaving**: that only reorders single items by type — Twins *juxtapose* two surface-different, same-structure items and demand the shared skeleton. Vs our **paraphrase eval**: that reworts *one* item; Twins use *two different* items with *maximally different* surface. Vs **R2's trap-pairs**: those minimize surface change to isolate one distractor axis (discrimination); Twins **maximize** surface change to isolate the shared **deep structure** (abstraction) — the manipulation Gick & Holyoak actually ran. Vs **R1's classification sprints**: those speed up an existing label; Twins **build the representation**.

- **Learning-theory basis.** Direct instantiation of analogical encoding / schema induction: two disparate analogs + active similarity-description → transferable schema, and the quality of the produced schema predicts transfer (Gick & Holyoak 1983; Loewenstein et al. 1999 ~3×; Gentner et al. 2003, 47% vs 6%). The **predict-before-tell** ordering is "a time for telling" (Schwartz & Bransford 1998). The **skeleton-articulation** step is prompted self-explanation (Bisra et al. 2018, g=0.55). The **skeleton itself** is evidence-tuned: Gick & Holyoak found a diagram/principle helps **only when paired with two analogs** — so we attach the skeleton exactly there, not to lone items.

- **Why LSAT-specific / differentiating.** Parallel-Reasoning/Parallel-Flaw/Method are *by definition* "match the structure, ignore the topic"; Flaw (the most common type) needs the abstract error named. Training cross-surface abstraction trains the literal task, and because *every* LR stimulus is an argument-with-a-structure, the schema transfers across all of LR (now ~2/3 of the score) and into RC argument questions.

- **How it works in our app (data/model/UI).**
  - `lsat/notetypes.py`: add an `LSAT Structure Twin` notetype to `_SPECS` — fields `stimulus_a, stimulus_b, structure_id, flaw_tag, shared_skeleton, skeleton_blanks, source_id, source_quote_a, source_quote_b, skill_tags`. Reuse the proven `LSAT Item` template pattern (render → `pycmd("lsatTwin:…")` → grade → reveal); factor the click/latency JS so Item and Twin share it. (Adding a notetype is additive; `ensure_notetypes` is idempotent.)
  - `lsat/events.py`: log twin attempts through the existing `append_event(...)` (append-only, HLC-synced). Store the produced-skeleton correctness and `structure_id` in `skill_tags` (node ids) so **no schema migration** is needed; RT is already first-class (`response_ms`).
  - `lsat/ai/` (reuse the injection-resistant pipeline): `generator.py` mints a twin **from a schema spec** — two instances on different topics + the shared skeleton + a **verbatim `source_quote` per stimulus** (the existing `quote not in source_text` substring check already drops fabricated/injected citations). The **independent** `checker.py` adds criteria: *both stimuli instantiate the SAME `structure_id`*, *their surface topics are genuinely different* (reuse `eval/leakage.py` tf-idf cosine ≤ a `TWIN_SURFACE_COS_MAX`), and *each span is verbatim*; fail-closed like today. `gold_set.py` + `validate_checker` gate the twin-validity **false-pass rate** (`config.CHECKER_FALSE_PASS_MAX`).
  - UI: a compare screen — a SvelteKit route `ts/routes/lsat-twins/` mirroring `ts/routes/lsat-dashboard/`, or a `qt/aqt/` page via `mediasrv`.

- **Implementation sketch (layer + effort).** `lsat/` + `lsat/ai/` + one study surface; **no Rust/proto for v1**. **Effort: M** (the AI generator/checker extension and the compare UI are the work; the data model is additive).

- **Risks / failure modes + how we measure transfer.**
  - *Content validity (top risk):* fabricating a pair that is surface-different but structurally *identical* is hard; a pair that secretly differs in structure teaches the wrong schema. Mitigation: independent checker's structure-match criterion + the surface-distance gate + human-checked `gold_set`; ship behind `card_check` (`CARD_CHECK_PASS_RATE_MIN`, `CARD_CHECK_WRONG_RATE_MAX`).
  - *Passive comparison does nothing* (Gick & Holyoak Part I): mitigate by requiring the produced skeleton/prediction before reveal (no skeleton → no credit).
  - *Overclaiming transfer* (Barnett & Ceci): we never assert it — we **measure** it via Feature 3.
  - **Transfer measurement:** primary = held-out **P(correct) on unseen, same-`structure_id`, high-surface-distance** items, plus the **memorization gap** (accuracy on near-surface minus far-surface) and the **structure slope** β (Feature 3). Add a `+twin` arm to `eval/ablation.py` (equal study time, paired synthetic learners, explicit ablatable `TWIN_BONUS`, `bootstrap_ci`), with the `leakage` cosine gate ensuring twins don't leak surface wording into the held-out set and the `ECE_MAX` calibration guard staying green. **Success = twin arm lifts far-surface transfer with a 95% CI excluding 0 and a flatter accuracy-vs-distance slope than controls.**

### Feature: Spot-the-Structure Sort (structure-vs-surface categorization — diagnostic *and* drill)

- **What it is (learner POV).** You're handed a small grid of ~9–12 short arguments **crossed on two axes**: K deep structures × M surface topics (e.g., 3 flaw structures × 3 topics). You drag them into groups "by what makes them work (or fail) the *same way*." The app then scores **whether you grouped by structure (expert) or by topic (novice)** and shows a single **Structure-Sort Index** (0 = pure surface, 1 = pure structure) with a trend line. As a **drill**, it then reveals the by-structure grouping and asks you to **self-explain the shared skeleton** of each cluster (and flags the topic-based lures you fell for). The point isn't the score screen; it's watching your index climb from "sorts by topic" to "sorts by argument form."

- **Why NOVEL vs generic Anki AND vs our built features.** Anki has **no categorization task at all**. Vs interleaving: interleaving controls queue order; it never asks the learner to categorize, and never measures *how* they represent items. Vs our taxonomy: we tag items by structure for the *system*; we have never asked whether the *student's* mental grouping is structural. **Measuring the novice→expert representational shift is, as far as I can find, absent from every LSAT product** — they report accuracy and timing, never representation.

- **Learning-theory basis.** Chi, Feltovich & Glaser (1981): sorting behavior *is* the classic operationalization of schema/expertise, and it moves toward structure with learning. Crossing structure × surface is the discriminative-contrast condition that aids induction and transfer (Kornell & Bjork 2008, cited in sibling memo R1). The reveal-then-explain step is self-explanation (Bisra et al. 2018).

- **Why LSAT-specific / differentiating.** The "same structure / different topic" grouping *is* the Parallel-Reasoning competence; the "different structure / same topic" foils are exactly the surface lures LSAC plants in Parallel/Flaw answer choices. So the index isn't a lab curiosity — it's a proxy for the exact discrimination the test rewards.

- **How it works in our app (data/model/UI).**
  - Reuse existing `LSAT Item`s: each already has `skill_tags` (structure/flaw) and a `stem` (surface topic). A sort session samples a **crossed** set: pick K `structure_id`s × M surface clusters. Surface clusters can be AI-labeled (checked) or derived by clustering stems with the **existing tf-idf vectors** (`eval/leakage.py` `_vec`/`_cosine`) — no new embedding stack.
  - `lsat/sort.py` (new, pure Python): build the crossed grid; score the learner's partition `P_u` against the structure partition `P_struct` and surface partition `P_surf`. **Index = ARI(P_u, P_struct) − ARI(P_u, P_surf)** (adjusted Rand), reported with an abstain gate (need ≥ N sorts, mirroring the readiness "give-up" contract in `lsat-taxonomy.yaml`).
  - Persistence: store each sort result as a derived snapshot; concurrent-device merge already has a rule — `lsat/events.py:resolve_lww` (HLC last-writer-wins) — or append raw sorts as events.
  - UI: a drag-drop panel + a dashboard trend via `lsat/dashboard_data.py` → `qt/aqt/lsat_dashboard.py` + `ts/routes/lsat-dashboard/`.

- **Implementation sketch (layer + effort).** Diagnostic v0 (score sorts over existing items, dashboard number) is **S**, pure Python + a small panel. Full drag-drop drill + trend is **M**. **No Rust/proto.**

- **Risks / failure modes + how we measure transfer.**
  - *Needs items crossed on structure×surface* (content requirement): mitigate with AI tag+generate behind the checker; seed a starter crossed set in `lsat/seed.py`.
  - *Noisy with few tiles / gameable:* require a minimum grid and ≥ N sorts before displaying (give-up gate); report a confidence band.
  - *Index construct-validity:* **this is itself a transfer measure** (structure-based sorting = far-transfer readiness, Chi 1981) — and we validate it in `eval/` by checking that a learner's Sort Index **correlates with held-out Parallel-Reasoning/Parallel-Flaw `P(correct)`** and **rises within-learner** across study (report Spearman ρ with `bootstrap_ci`; a null correlation parks the feature — the honest negative the harness is designed to surface).

### Feature: Schema Transfer Meter (memorization-vs-transfer readout + the cross-surface transfer eval)

- **What it is (learner POV).** On the dashboard, each argument-structure schema gets a two-state honesty badge: **"Memorized here"** (you're right only on familiar topics) vs **"Transferred"** (you're right on brand-new topics too). It's the same "don't flatter the user" ethos as our readiness contract — it refuses to call a schema learned until you've shown it on **new surface**. Under the hood this is the measurement layer that grades Features 1–2.

- **Why NOVEL vs generic Anki AND vs our built features.** It extends `eval/paraphrase.py` from *same-item rewording* to **cross-item, same-structure, surface-distance-graded** transfer — a memorization-vs-transfer separation **per argument schema** that neither Anki nor (to my knowledge) any LSAT product reports. It also turns "did a schema form?" (Gick & Holyoak's construct) into a behavioral, logged number.

- **Learning-theory basis.** Barnett & Ceci (2002): measure transfer along explicit distance dimensions, not as one number → we report a **curve over surface distance**, controlling difficulty. Gick & Holyoak (1983): schema quality predicts transfer → the meter is the observable proxy for schema quality.

- **How it works in our app (data/model/UI).**
  - Tag items with `structure_id` (existing tag machinery). Define **surface distance** `d = 1 − cosine_surface(item, seen_items)` using the **existing tf-idf** vectors (`eval/leakage.py`), with logical-connective/stem-cue tokens down-weighted so distance reflects *topic*, not *form*.
  - `eval/transfer.py` (new, report-only first — same stance as `paraphrase.py`): for each `structure_id`, fit `P(correct) ~ ability + β·d (+ difficulty)`; report **far-distance accuracy**, the **memorization gap** (near − far), and the **slope β** with `bootstrap_ci`. β≈0 ⇒ structural transfer; β≪0 ⇒ surface-bound. Wire the `+twin`/`+sort` arms of `eval/ablation.py` to this primary metric.

```text
memorization_gap(S) = P(correct | S, d in NEAR) − P(correct | S, d in FAR)
transfer_index(S)   = P(correct | S, d in FAR)          # high = transferred
structure_slope(S)  = β in logistic P(correct) ~ ability + β·d + difficulty
```

  - Dashboard: a per-schema badge in `lsat/dashboard_data.py` → `qt/aqt/lsat_dashboard.py` + `ts/routes/lsat-dashboard/`, abstaining until enough new-surface items exist (give-up gate).

- **Implementation sketch (layer + effort).** Mostly `eval/` (Python) reusing `bootstrap_ci`, the leakage cosine, and the ablation harness, plus a dashboard readout. **Effort: S–M. No Rust/proto.**

- **Risks / failure modes + how we measure transfer.** Cold-start (need same-schema items across enough surfaces → give-up gate). Bag-of-words surface distance is a **proxy** — shared logical-marker words could inflate similarity; mitigate by content-word IDF weighting and note it as a limitation. **Difficulty×distance confound** (far-surface items might just be harder): control with the within-schema `difficulty` field already on `LSAT Item`. Synthetic-learner caveat (as the whole harness documents): this proves the **methodology** — a distance-graded, equal-time, CI'd transfer curve — pending a real A/B.

## Threats to validity (what a debater should attack — and my answers)

- **"This is just R2's contrasting cases / R1's minimal pairs."** No — opposite manipulation. R2/R1 **minimize** surface variation to sharpen one discrimination (trap axis / fine category boundary). Twins **maximize** surface variation to abstract the shared **structure**. Gick & Holyoak's effect is defined over *semantically disparate* analogs; my design is the faithful one for schema induction, and Feature 3 measures a different construct (transfer across topic) than R2's trap-susceptibility.
- **"Far transfer barely exists."** Conceded for *true* far transfer (Barnett & Ceci). But LSAT cross-*topic* transfer is **near** on 8 of 9 Barnett–Ceci dimensions (same domain, format, modality, timeframe); the only gap is surface content. I target that gradient and **measure the curve**, never a binary claim.
- **"The eval is synthetic."** True today (as `eval/ablation.py` documents). I ship **pre-registered** designs with **ablatable** bonus parameters (`TWIN_BONUS`) and the same honesty stance as `INTERLEAVE_BONUS`; the defensible claim is "sound methodology + large adjacent-domain effects," not "proven on LSAT students yet."
- **"AI can't reliably make true structural twins."** The biggest real risk. Guarded by the independent, fail-closed checker (structure-match + surface-distance + verbatim-span), the `gold_set` false-pass gate, and human spot-checks — the same defenses that already gate our card pipeline.
