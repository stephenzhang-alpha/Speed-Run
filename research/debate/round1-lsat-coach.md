## 1. Priorities — what actually moves an LSAT score

Twenty years of score reports tell me the same story. Points are lost, in order, to:

1. **Pacing.** Running out of time is the #1 score killer. A 168-capable student
   who leaves 4 questions blank and rushes 6 more is a 161. Two of three scored
   sections are now LR at ~1:24/question (M4 `[market-grounding]`), so pacing on LR
   is *doubly* decisive. Nothing on this list matters if the clock beats you.
2. **Trap answers.** The LSAT is best-of-five with *engineered* wrong answers —
   too-strong, out-of-scope, reversal, half-right, sufficient↔necessary. Missed
   points are overwhelmingly *predictable pulls toward a manufactured distractor*
   (R2's whole thesis), not blank-slate ignorance. The high scorer's habit is
   "why is each of the other four wrong," every time.
3. **Confident, repeated mistakes.** The same student falls for the same trap on
   the same question type, week after week, *sure they're right*. Those confident
   errors are where the points hide — and, happily, where correction pays off most.
4. **Fragile knowledge that cracks under the clock.** Plenty of students can get
   an item right untimed and blow it timed. That is a *different disease* than not
   knowing it, and it needs a *different medicine*.

### The distinction the whole debate hinges on: timed vs. untimed

This is the most important thing I'll say. **Learning a skill and executing it
under a stopwatch are two different jobs, and features must declare which one they
serve.** The research backs me hard here: Soderstrom & Bjork (M1) show current
timed *performance* is an unreliable index of durable *learning*; the entire
7Sage Blind Review method (B) exists precisely to *separate untimed fundamentals
from timed technique*.

- **Untimed learning mode** is where you're allowed to be slow, effortful, even to
  fail on purpose. Schema-building, self-explanation, worked examples, contrasting
  cases live here. Friction is acceptable *here* because you're building the tool.
- **Timed execution mode** is where the only currency is correct-answers-per-minute
  under pressure. Here, friction is the enemy. **This is where I break with the
  academics:** heavy typing / written self-explanation *during timed practice* is
  poison — it corrupts the very pacing signal we need and no real student sustains it.

A feature that blurs these two modes is a bug. A feature that names its mode and
respects it is a keeper.

## 3. Ranked verdict — CHAMPION / CONDITIONAL / CUT

Judged on **expected timed-score impact × real-world adherence under pressure**,
with a note on where the research backs my gut and where it slaps it down.

### CHAMPION (build these)

- **C1 — Trap Profile. CHAMPION.** My #1, and it should be everyone's. It is the
  *cheapest high-impact feature on the board*: the chosen letter is already posted
  to Python and then discarded (M1, R2 both flag this) — we're literally throwing
  away the single most diagnostic byte in the app. It targets the student's
  *specific recurring mistakes*, which is the whole job of coaching. **Evidence
  backs my gut:** a chosen distractor is a *diagnosis*, not noise (Sadler 1998,
  R2), and which distractor you pick is formally modelable (Thissen 1989, R2).
  Near-zero friction. Do it first; everything else feeds on it.

- **C4 — Blind Review Loop. CHAMPION.** This is the signature technique of *every*
  high scorer I've produced, and it is the literal implementation of my timed-vs-
  untimed priority. It answers the one question that decides a study plan: *"Do you
  not know this, or did you just run out of time?"* The 2×2 (pressure / knowledge /
  fragile / mastered) is exactly how I triage a student. It also makes our mastery
  *honest* — today a lucky timed guess counts as mastery and suppresses a topic
  that still needs work (B nails this). **Honest evidence note:** Blind Review
  itself is *community best-practice, not an RCT* (B says so plainly). My gut rates
  it gold; the literature only rates the *mechanisms* underneath it
  (Kruger–Dunning, Metcalfe, Dunlosky) as strong. I'll own that gap — but I've
  watched it work too many times to rank it below a lab-clean feature that doesn't
  survive contact with a stopwatch.

- **C6 — Hypercorrection Queue. CHAMPION.** Confident errors on the LSAT are almost
  always *trap captures* — you were sure because the trap was well-built (R2). This
  is the highest-ROI review target that exists, and the evidence is genuinely
  strong: Butterfield & Metcalfe (M1/M3/R2), confident errors are hypercorrected.
  **Critical caveat the research forces on me:** confident errors *come back* if
  you don't re-practice them (Butler, Fazio & Marsh, M3) — so the *spacing* is not
  optional, and this is exactly what our FSRS layer is for. Cheap (Python-only,
  reuses the queue). It needs C5's one-tap confidence capture as a prerequisite —
  which I accept, because one tap is not friction.

- **C7 — Pacing Engine. CHAMPION.** Pacing is priority #1, so this is non-
  negotiable. A faithful **current-format** simulator (2LR+1RC, 35-min ramp) stops
  students drilling the wrong section counts or the retired Logic Games, trains the
  skip-and-return / guess-and-move policy that actually banks points, and the
  **Choke Index** (relaxed − timed accuracy) separates *choking* from *ignorance*.
  **Evidence backs my gut:** pressure eats the working memory the ablest rely on
  (Beilock & Carr, M3), and practicing to automaticity removes choking (Beilock
  2004, M3). I fold M3's per-item choke work into M4's section simulator because
  they're the same problem at two zoom levels. The **opt-in anxiety-writing** tool
  stays *opt-in and measured only* — Ramirez & Beilock got d=0.57 but Myers 2021
  replicated to nothing (M3), so I won't promise it, I'll test it.

### CONDITIONAL (I'll take it if the condition is met — but I won't headline it)

- **C2 — Trap Autopsy + Spaced Distractor-Rejection. CONDITIONAL.** I *love* the
  "why is each wrong answer wrong" habit — it's canonical. **Condition:** during
  *timed* practice it must be a one-tap *menu* ("which trap got you?"), never
  free-text; the *written* autopsy lives only in Blind Review (untimed). The spaced
  distractor-rejection cards are strong (competitive-MC retrieval, Little/Bjork,
  M1) **only if** the AI can generate valid ones (gate on the existing checker).
  Met those conditions and it's nearly a champion; ignore them and it's homework.

- **C3 — Minimal-Pair Trap Contrasts. CONDITIONAL.** Theoretically the sharpest
  trap trainer — it drills the exact discriminations LSAC engineers (sufficient vs.
  necessary, strengthen vs. justify). **Condition:** content validity. A pair that
  *secretly* differs on two axes teaches the wrong lesson (R1/R2 both rate this the
  highest-risk build). Ship it only after the checker + gold-set prove valid single-
  axis pairs, and steer it with C1 so we drill *your* traps.

- **C5 — Confidence Calibration. CONDITIONAL (capture = yes, dashboard = later).**
  The one-tap capture is a prerequisite for C6, so it's in. The full reliability/
  resolution *dashboard* is a nice honesty mirror but not itself a score-mover —
  build the capture now, the dashboard when there's time. Keep it one tap, sampled;
  the moment it's a slider mid-section it's dead.

- **C8 — Structure Sprints. CONDITIONAL → (in my final set).** Expert LR advice is
  literally "classify the question type in under 3 seconds." This trains that, in
  seconds-long game-like trials — *the opposite of friction*, so adherence is
  excellent, and it attacks the *root cause* of slowness (non-automatic
  recognition), feeding pacing. **Condition / honest evidence note:** the big PLM
  effects are from *aviation/medical/math* (Kellman, R1); transfer to LSAT is
  *inferred, not proven*. It trains a sub-skill, not the whole item. I include it
  because low-risk + high-adherence + right target, but it's my most provisional
  champion.

- **C9 — Fluency Gates. CONDITIONAL.** "Accurate but slow" is the exact mid-160s
  plateau; gating retirement on speed-and-accuracy (ARTS, R1) targets it. Condition:
  guard against RT gaming (accuracy co-criterion + timeout) and don't let it wreck
  calibration. Natural complement to the Pacing Engine.

- **C10 — Structure Twins. CONDITIONAL (this is where I concede to the scientist).**
  The single best-evidenced way to *build* a transferable schema (Gick & Holyoak;
  Gentner ~3×, 47% vs 6% — M2/C). Parallel/Method/Flaw are *literally* structure-
  matching, which is now ~2/3 of the test. **But** it's slow, effortful, and purely
  *untimed learning* — it never touches execution. Condition: keep it in learning
  mode, gate it behind the timed features, and beat the content-generation risk.
  Phase-2, not a headline.

- **C11 — Faded Parallels. CONDITIONAL.** Good medicine for the Parallel/Method
  plateau (worked-example effect, M2). Condition: adaptive entry to dodge expertise-
  reversal, and the authoring cost is real. Untimed learning mode.

- **C13 — Schema Transfer Meter. CONDITIONAL.** An honesty layer ("memorized here"
  vs. "transferred"), not an intervention. Only worth it if C10/C11 ship — it's the
  scoreboard for them.

- **C14 — Pattern Mastery Loops. CONDITIONAL.** Successive relearning is a durable-
  learning heavyweight (Rawson, ~80% at 1 week, M1). Condition: it's *time-costly*
  (the time-matched effect shrinks to d≈0.28, M1 concedes) — cap concurrent loops so
  it doesn't eat the prep window.

- **C15 — Prime-then-Read. CONDITIONAL (RC-first).** Pretesting is well-replicated
  (Pan & Carpenter, M1) and RC is a growing third of the test. Condition: it does
  *not* generalize to untested material (M1's own caveat), and fail-first-then-read
  adds session time — so target it precisely, RC passages first.

- **C18 — ZPD Item Selector. CONDITIONAL (cheap, do it, but it's plumbing).** It
  fixes a genuine defect the research exposed and *changed my mind on*: our queue
  serves the *hardest* card in the *weakest* topic, but Metcalfe & Kornell's region
  of proximal learning says most learning happens at *medium* difficulty (M4).
  Small Python re-rank toward ~85%. Not a headline, but yes.

### CUT (don't build, or demote to a config knob / park)

- **C21 — CDM Attribute Profile (DINA/G-DINA). CUT — my single strongest cut.** See
  §"strongest cut" below. Latent-attribute psychometrics on one sparse solo log is
  machinery in search of data.

- **C20 — BKT Mastery. CUT.** Cheap to *code*, worthless without *data*. A single
  user's per-skill sequences are far too short to fit 4 parameters; the memo itself
  concedes identifiability problems and "abstain below a floor" (M4) — i.e., for
  most students most of the time it *abstains and does nothing*. Recency-weighted
  accuracy plus C1/C6 gets us the actionable 90%.

- **C19 — Adaptive Measurement Probe. CUT.** It *measures*, it doesn't *teach*, and
  serving a stream of P≈0.5 items is demoralizing by design. Its only payoff is a
  tighter readiness *number* — I don't spend a student's scarce, stressed prep hours
  to shrink a confidence interval.

- **C22 — Mastery-Gated Advanced Types. CUT.** Over-gating starves the coverage the
  readiness model needs and hurts completion (Kulik's own caveat, M4). Also depends
  on C20/C21, which I'm cutting.

- **C12 — Skeleton Builder. CUT as a headline (park as infrastructure).** Argument-
  mapping evidence is quasi-experimental and advocate-led (van Gelder, M2 rates it
  MODERATE and discounts it), the drag-drop UI is the biggest build here, and
  *mapping ≠ answering under time*. If the Structure features get greenlit it can
  ride along as auto-tagging; on its own, no.

- **C16 — Commit-First Drills. CUT (park).** Productive failure has real evidence
  for conceptual material (Sinha & Kapur, M1) — I'll concede that to the scientist —
  but it adds friction/time to *every* skill intro and its boundary conditions are
  sharp (nothing for procedural knowledge). Not a timed-execution feature; low
  score-per-effort.

- **C17 — Delayed Feedback Knob. CUT as a feature (keep as a setting).** The
  evidence is genuinely *contested* — Butler says delayed can win, Kulik says
  classrooms favor immediate (M1). My coaching gut says give feedback *now* in
  practice. Don't build a headline around a coin-flip; expose it as a config knob
  and let the eval harness pick a default.

- **C23 — DP Micro-Loops. CUT (park).** It's "targeted drilling," which C1 + the
  queue already deliver. Redundant.

- **C24 — SRL Loop. CUT (park).** Forethought→reflection is fine in theory but on
  its own it's score-neutral reflection theater; the pieces that matter (confidence
  capture, attribution routing) already live inside C4/C5/C7.

## 4. My straw-man final set (maximize score gain per unit of student effort)

Five features, spanning both modes, forming one closed loop: **diagnose → decompose
→ execute → correct → automate.**

| Feature | Mode | Why it's in |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Trap Profile (C1)** | Built from **timed** data; surfaced for **untimed** review | The substrate. Free (we already discard the data), maximally LSAT-native, targets *your* recurring mistakes. |
| **2. Blind Review Loop (C4)** | The **mode-switch engine itself** (timed pass → untimed pass) | Separates "don't know" from "too slow." Signature high-scorer technique; makes mastery honest. Carries a one-tap trap-menu autopsy (C2-lite). |
| **3. Pacing Engine (C7)** | **Timed execution** | Attacks the #1 score killer on the current format; Choke Index isolates choking from ignorance. |
| **4. Hypercorrection Queue (C6)** | **Untimed learning** (spaced re-test) | Fixes confident trap-misses — the highest-ROI points — and *keeps* them fixed via spacing. Needs C5's one-tap confidence. |
| **5. Structure Sprints (C8)** | **Untimed learning** (automaticity) | Low-friction, game-like; drives down recognition time, which is the root of most pacing failures. My most provisional pick. |

**How they chain:** the Pacing Engine + Blind Review generate *timed* data → the
Trap Profile + Confidence capture turn that into a personal weakness map → the
Hypercorrection Queue drills the confident trap-misses on fresh spaced items →
Structure Sprints grind recognition to automaticity so the next timed pass is
faster. Every minute of student effort routes to a named, measured weakness.

**Top upgrade if we can trust AI generation:** swap/add **Minimal-Pair Trap
Contrasts (C3)** — it's the most surgical trap trainer, gated on content validity.
**Concession slot for the scientist:** if we fund a phase-2 *learning* module,
**Structure Twins (C10)** is the one I'd back on the evidence.

## 5. Pre-emptive critiques (and my rebuttals)

### The Cognitive Scientist will say I'm anti-intellectual
**Their case:** "Champion Structure Twins (C10), Skeleton Builder (C12), self-
explanation (C2), productive failure (C16), pretesting (C15), successive relearning
(C14) — these have meta-analytic effect sizes; the coach is just cutting anything
that looks like school."

**My rebuttal:** I'm not anti-evidence — I *ranked by* the memos' effect sizes and
I've conceded Structure Twins and productive failure on their merits. But (a) most
of your darlings are **untimed skill-builders that never touch execution**, and the
LSAT scores execution under a clock (your own Soderstrom & Bjork, M1: performance ≠
learning cuts *both* ways). (b) **Heavy typing / written self-explanation during
timed practice destroys the pacing signal** — and even your literature isn't clean:
Barbieri 2023 found self-explanation prompts *negatively* moderated the worked-
example effect (M2 flags this honestly). (c) Big lab effect sizes come from
*controlled* dosing; a stressed solo student three weeks out won't dose them.
Verdict: your features are welcome — *in untimed learning mode, gated behind the
timed loop*, not as headliners.

### The Engineer will say I'm ignoring feasibility
**Their case:** "The cheap wins are BKT, the calibration dashboard, ZPD re-rank,
fluency gates — all Python-only, no Rust, reuse existing plumbing. The Pacing Engine
and Blind Review are effort-L UI builds. Ship what's cheap."

**My rebuttal:** *Cheap to code ≠ valuable.* BKT and CDM are cheap precisely because
they're low-parameter — and low-parameter models **starve on one user's sparse log**
and abstain (your own M4 says so). Don't let "no Rust change" become the selection
criterion; that's optimizing for the wrong variable. Meanwhile the two features I
most want happen to be a *win-win*: **C1 Trap Profile is nearly free** (we already
receive and discard the chosen letter — go get it), and **C6 Hypercorrection is
Python-only** and reuses the queue. Yes, the Pacing Engine is effort-L — pay for it,
because it's the single highest-score-impact build on the board. We buy *points*,
not *diffs*.

### The Student-Advocate will say I'm too harsh
**Their case:** "Blind Review doubles session time, hard drills demoralize, the mini-
CAT feels awful, trap-hunting is punitive — build gentle SRL reflection, anxiety
tools, encouragement. Adherence is everything."

**My rebuttal:** Adherence *is* everything — which is why I already **cut the
demoralizing mini-CAT (C19)** and refused to over-gate (C22), and why my 5th pick is
a *game-like, low-friction* sprint, not a grind. But you get adherence from **visible
score gains and fast, winnable drills**, not from reflection theater (C24) or a
worry-journal whose evidence collapsed on replication (Myers 2021, M3 — that's why I
kept it opt-in-and-measured, not promised). Blind Review is hard, but it's *scoped*
(flagged/low-confidence items only) and it's *the* thing that works — I won't soften
it into uselessness. The kindest thing I can do for a student is get them the points.
