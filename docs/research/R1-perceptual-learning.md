# R1 — Perceptual Learning, Pattern Recognition & Automaticity for LSAT Prep

_Research memo · Round 1 · to be attacked in debate_

**Persona.** I am a perceptual-learning & cognitive-automaticity scientist (Kellman/Gibson/Goldstone lineage). My claim is narrow and testable: elite LSAT performance is less about _knowing more rules_ and more about _seeing structure faster_ — fluent, low-effort recognition of argument/question form. That is precisely the class of skill that Perceptual Learning Modules (PLMs) and Adaptive Response-Time-based Sequencing (ARTS) were built to accelerate, and it is the one dimension the current fork logs (`response_ms`) but does **not** yet train or reward.

---

## Lens & why it matters

Traditional instruction (and most LSAT prep) teaches **declarative** knowledge — "here is what a Necessary Assumption is; here are the five wrong-answer families." But cross-domain studies of expertise show that what separates experts from novices is largely **perceptual**: experts _extract the relevant structure_ from a stimulus quickly, selectively, and with little cognitive load, while novices laboriously reason it out feature by feature (Kellman & Garrigan, 2009; Chase & Simon, 1973). Two mechanisms matter here:

1. **Discovery / selection** — learning _which_ features carry the diagnostic signal (conclusion vs. premise, causal vs. correlational claim, sufficient vs. necessary conditional) and ignoring surface distractors.
2. **Fluency / automaticity** — the same classification getting _faster and lower-effort_ with practice, freeing working memory for the hard part of the item (Shiffrin & Schneider, 1977; Logan, 1988).

The post-2024 LSAT sharpens the case: Analytical Reasoning (Logic Games) is gone; the scored exam is **two Logical Reasoning sections + one Reading Comprehension section**, so LR is now ~2/3 of the score, at ~35 minutes for 24–26 questions each (LSAC, 2024). LR is a _speeded_ structural-classification task under time pressure — the exact profile PLM/ARTS methods target. Perceptual-learning interventions in comparably complex domains (aviation, chemistry, mathematics, medical imaging) repeatedly produce **large, durable** gains in _both accuracy and speed_ from **short** training (often < 1–2 hours), and they transfer to novel items (see Evidence base). No mainstream LSAT tool trains this dimension directly; they drill full, timed questions and _report_ analytics. That gap is the opportunity.

> Scope guard: perceptual training is powerful but not magic. In a hard radiology discrimination it produced rapid gains yet did **not** reach expert level, and several training paradigms tied (Cognitive Research: Principles & Implications, 2023). So I treat PLMs as a _fluency accelerator layered on top of_ the existing content/FSRS system, not a replacement, and attach measurable, falsifiable hypotheses to each feature.

## Evidence base

All URLs verified live during this research pass (2026-06-30). Effect sizes are quoted as reported by the source; where a number comes from a review's summary table I say so.

- **Kellman, P. J., & Garrigan, P. (2009). "Perceptual learning and human expertise." _Physics of Life Reviews_, 6, 53–84.** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.plrev.2008.12.001 · open copy: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6198797/
  _Finding:_ Theoretical anchor. Defines perceptual learning (PL) as experience-induced change in _information extraction_, distinguishes **discovery** effects (finding diagnostic structure) from **fluency** effects (faster, lower-load extraction), and argues PL is a fundamental, under-taught driver of expertise in high-level symbolic domains (chess, mathematics, reading). _LSAT relevance:_ legitimizes treating "seeing the argument's structure" as a trainable perceptual skill distinct from declarative rule-knowledge — the core premise of every feature below.

- **Kellman, P. J., & Kaiser, M. K. (1994). "Perceptual Learning Modules in Flight Training." _Proc. Human Factors & Ergonomics Society Annual Meeting_, 38(18), 1183–1187.** https://doi.org/10.1177/154193129403801808
  _Finding / design:_ Speeded forced-choice classification PLMs (read an instrument panel → classify flight attitude; view terrain → pick chart location), 9 blocks of trials, speed **and** accuracy measured. After **1–2 hours**, non-pilots were **as accurate as and reliably faster than 500–2,500-hour pilots before training**; experienced pilots cut instrument-interpretation time **~60%**. _LSAT relevance:_ the archetype for a speeded structural-classification drill; shows huge accuracy+speed gains from short practice on a complex real-world "read the display, name the state" task — directly analogous to "read the stimulus, name the question type / flaw / structure."

- **Kellman, P. J., Massey, C. M., & Son, J. Y. (2010). "Perceptual Learning Modules in Mathematics: Enhancing Students' Pattern Recognition, Structure Extraction, and Fluency." _Topics in Cognitive Science_, 2(2), 285–305.** https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1756-8765.2009.01053.x
  _Finding:_ Three classroom PLMs in symbolic math. The **Algebraic Transformations PLM** trained students to _classify_ whether two expressions are related by a valid transformation (never to solve) and produced **dramatic gains in the speed of later equation solving**; the Linear Measurement and MultiRep PLMs transferred to novel problems, with **no decrement at delayed posttest** (durable). _LSAT relevance:_ proves PL works on _symbolic, relational_ material (like LSAT conditionals and argument structure) and that training pure _classification/fluency_ transfers to full downstream problem-solving — the mechanism behind "Structure Sprints."

- **Kellman, P. J. (2013). "Adaptive and Perceptual Learning Technologies in Medical Education and Training." _Military Medicine_, 178(10):98.** doi:10.7205/MILMED-D-13-00218 · PDF: https://kellmanlab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/357/2025/09/kellman_2013.pdf
  _Finding:_ Describes the **ARTS** system (below) and reports medical PALMs. Year-1 **dermatology**: accuracy 66%→84% (**Cohen's d = 1.55**), response time roughly halved (**d = 1.00**); **histopathology**: accuracy +~12 pts (**d ≈ 0.90–0.98**), RT down sharply (**d ≈ 0.95–1.19**). Summarizes math PLM effect sizes in the range **d = 0.84–2.69**. Memorable design principle: _"there are no slow, good surgeons"_ — **fluency is used both to optimize learning and as a goal of learning.** _LSAT relevance:_ strongest single source that adaptive + perceptual training yields large, durable gains in _diagnostic pattern recognition_ while explicitly treating **speed as a mastery goal**, not just accuracy.

- **Mettler, E., Massey, C. M., & Kellman, P. J. (2016). "A comparison of adaptive and fixed schedules of practice." _Journal of Experimental Psychology: General_, 145(7).** https://doi.org/10.1037/xge0000170
  _Finding:_ **ARTS** (spacing driven by each learner's accuracy **and response time**) beat _both_ fixed expanding and fixed equal spacing at immediate and delayed tests; **yoked** controls (same average spacing, replayed) did _not_ capture the benefit — so the gain comes specifically from adapting to the individual item × learner in real time. _LSAT relevance:_ the empirical warrant for making RT a first-class scheduling signal ("Fluency Gates"), over and above fixed/accuracy-only spacing.

- **Mettler, E., Massey, C. M., & Kellman, P. J. (2014). "Adaptive response-time-based category sequencing in perceptual learning." _Vision Research_, 99, 111–123.** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.visres.2013.12.009
  _Finding / design:_ Applies ARTS to **perceptual category learning** (butterfly taxonomy) with paired-comparison trials and **dual accuracy+speed mastery criteria** (a category is retired after accurate responses to spaced exemplars **under a criterion RT**). The priority score governing re-presentation is `P_i = a(N_i − D)[b(1 − α_i)·log(RT_i / r) + α_i·W]` (α = error indicator, N = trials since last seen, D = enforced delay, W = error penalty). Learning transferred to **novel** category exemplars. _LSAT relevance:_ a concrete, portable algorithm and mastery definition for LSAT structure "categories," and evidence that PL transfers to unseen instances (not memorized items).

- **Kellman, P. J., Massey, C. M., Krasne, S., & Mettler, E. (2023). "Adaptive Perceptual Learning and Signal Detection Theory in Skin Cancer Screening." _Proc. Cognitive Science Society (CogSci)_.** PDF: https://kellmanlab.psych.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/357/2025/09/kellman_massey_krasne_mettler_2023.pdf
  _Finding:_ Adaptive PL for classifying skin lesions, training **< 30 min** on average. All adaptive conditions beat conventional instruction with **very large effect sizes (Cohen's d ≈ 2.24–2.53 immediate, 2.32–2.68 delayed on learning efficiency)**. Novel result: **SDT-based retirement** — using a running **d′** (which counts _false alarms_, i.e., "this looks like category X but isn't") rather than raw accuracy — reached equal accuracy with **less learning time** (medium effect vs. standard retirement). _LSAT relevance:_ motivates the "Minimal-Pair Contrast Drills" feature: LSAT trap answers are false-alarm engineering, and d′ (not accuracy) is the right mastery metric for confusable categories.

- **Chase, W. G., & Simon, H. A. (1973). "Perception in chess." _Cognitive Psychology_, 4(1), 55–81.** doi:10.1016/0010-0285(73)90004-2 · open copy: https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/chess/1973-chase.pdf
  _Finding:_ Masters and novices search _similar_ numbers of moves; masters differ by **rapidly perceiving meaningful chunks** (near-perfect recall of real positions, but _not_ random ones). Expertise ≈ a large vocabulary of recognized patterns (later, ~50k chunks; Gobet & Simon template theory, https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/chess/1996-gobet.pdf). _LSAT relevance:_ the canonical demonstration that elite performance in a reasoning domain is _pattern recognition_, not more search — the theoretical basis for chunking argument structures.

- **Kornell, N., & Bjork, R. A. (2008). "Learning concepts and categories: Is spacing the 'enemy of induction'?" _Psychological Science_, 19(6), 585–592.** https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2008.02127.x
  _Finding:_ **Interleaving** exemplars of different categories (artists' painting styles) beat massing for _inductive_ category learning and transfer to new exemplars — via **discriminative contrast** — even though learners _felt_ massing worked better (a fluency illusion). _LSAT relevance:_ the fork already interleaves _question types_; this justifies pushing further into **minimal-pair contrast** between confusable categories, and warns that students will mis-rate the hard-but-effective drills.

- **Shiffrin, R. M., & Schneider, W. (1977). "Controlled and automatic human information processing: II." _Psychological Review_, 84(2), 127–190** (Part I: 84(1), 1–66). https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.84.2.127 · **Logan, G. D. (1988). "Toward an instance theory of automatization." _Psychological Review_, 95(4), 492–527.** PDF: http://www.psy.vanderbilt.edu/faculty/logan/1988LoganPR.pdf
  _Finding:_ **Consistent stimulus→response mapping** over practice yields **automatic processing** (fast, parallel, low-load), vs. controlled processing (slow, serial, capacity-limited). Logan: automatization is a shift from step-by-step _computation_ to single-step _memory retrieval_, predicting power-law RT speed-ups — so **RT is the observable readout of automaticity**. _LSAT relevance:_ grounds using response time as the mastery signal and defines what "fluent" means mechanistically; LSAT question types are a _consistent mapping_ (stem cues → type), i.e., exactly the condition under which automaticity develops.

- **"Evaluating the effectiveness of different perceptual training methods in a difficult visual discrimination task with ultrasound images." _Cognitive Research: Principles and Implications_, 8:19 (2023).** https://doi.org/10.1186/s41235-023-00467-0
  _Finding (honest caveat):_ On a genuinely hard graded discrimination (hepatic-steatosis severity), perceptual training improved novices **rapidly but not to expert level**, and standard vs. comparison vs. annotated/step-wise paradigms performed **similarly**. _LSAT relevance:_ bounds expectations — PLMs accelerate fluency and lift accuracy, but won't alone manufacture a 180; and the specific training _format_ may matter less than _dose × adaptivity_. This is the caveat I would concede in debate.

**Applied / market & task context (used for novelty and LSAT-specificity, not as cognitive-science evidence):** LSAC format change, Aug 2024 (https://www.lsac.org/blog/what-to-expect-starting-with-august-2024-lsat); LSAT Demon's per-sub-type _timing_ tracking + adaptive drilling (https://practicetestgeeks.com/lsat/lsat-demon); 7Sage analytics + custom drills (https://7sage.com/); Khan Academy's official prep retired to LSAC LawHub drill sets (https://testpreppal.com/lsat/question-bank/lsac); expert LR advice to "classify the question type in < 3 s" and "find the conclusion first" (https://lsathacks.com/guide/faq/how-to-go-faster-logical-reasoning/, https://www.kaptest.com/study/lsat/guide-to-lsat-logical-reasoning/).

## Candidate features

Shared substrate I build on (already in the fork): the append-only event log `lsat/events.py` already records `response_ms` per graded answer, and `fold_recent_performance` collapses events into a **recency-weighted accuracy** per taxonomy node. Crucially, **RT is captured but only used as a covariate** inside the performance model (`lsat/models/performance.py`: `P(correct)=σ(ability + w0 + w1·difficulty + w2·timing_z)`) — it never drives _mastery_, _retirement_, or the _points-at-stake_ queue (`rslib/src/scheduler/points_at_stake.rs`, which blends `0.5·recall + 0.5·perf_mastery`). Every feature below turns that latent RT signal into a training and scheduling signal.

### Feature 1 — Structure Sprints (a speeded structural-classification PLM)

**(a) Student-facing.** A fast, game-like drill mode. You see _only_ a stimulus (an LR argument, an RC paragraph, or a bare question stem) and one small fixed set of buttons, and you tap the structural category as fast as you can — e.g. _"What is being asked?"_ (Weaken / Strengthen / Assumption / Flaw / Inference / Parallel / Principle…), _"Where is the conclusion?"_ (first sentence / last sentence / middle / unstated), _"What's the flaw family?"_ (causal / sampling / equivocation / ad hominem…), or _"Premise or conclusion?"_ on a highlighted sentence. Trials last seconds, not minutes; you get instant right/wrong **and your response time**, a running "fluency" meter, and the session keeps serving the categories you're slow or wrong on. No answer choices, no solving — this trains _seeing_, not _doing_.

**(b) Mechanism + citations.** This is the classic PLM primitive: **many short classification trials with varied exemplars and immediate feedback**, which builds fluent information extraction and automaticity (Kellman & Garrigan, 2009). The direct precedent is the aviation instrument-panel PLM — a speeded "read the display → classify the state" task where novices matched 1,000-hour pilots after 1–2 hours (Kellman & Kaiser, 1994) — and the Algebraic Transformations PLM, where training pure _classification_ of symbolic structure sped up downstream problem-solving and transferred to novel items (Kellman, Massey & Son, 2010). Because LSAT stem-cue → question-type is a **consistent mapping**, repeated speeded practice is exactly the condition that yields automatic (fast, low-load) processing (Shiffrin & Schneider, 1977; Logan, 1988), and the point of automaticity is to free working memory for the genuinely hard reasoning in the answer choices. Chunking theory (Chase & Simon, 1973) predicts that with enough varied reps, students stop parsing word-by-word and start recognizing whole argument templates.

**(c) Novelty.** _vs. the current app:_ the only graded task today is a full multiple-choice `LSAT Item` (~60–120 s); there is **no** sub-question speeded classification, and the logged `response_ms` is never trained down. _vs. the market:_ 7Sage, LSAT Demon, Kaplan/PowerScore, and LawHub all drill **whole timed questions** organized by type and _report_ analytics; LSAT Demon even shows per-sub-type timing curves — but none isolate a **speeded structural-classification micro-task** as its own trainer. Extracting the perception step into a seconds-long, RT-scored PLM is, as far as I can find, unique in LSAT prep.

**(d) LSAT-specificity.** Expert LR advice literally is "classify the question type in under three seconds" and "find the conclusion first" (LSAThacks; Kaplan). Parallel-Reasoning and Parallel-Flaw questions are _pure_ structure-matching. With two LR sections now ≈ 50% of the score and ~85 s/question, the difference between a 165 and a 175 is largely whether type/structure recognition is automatic. The drill reuses the taxonomy's existing `stem_cues` and `flaw_catalog` as the category label sets.

**(e) Implementation sketch (this fork).**

- **`lsat/` (new `lsat/plm.py`):** builds a sprint session — pick a _dimension_ (question_type | conclusion_location | premise_vs_conclusion | flaw_family), assemble stimuli + the small candidate label set from `lsat/taxonomy.py`, and order trials adaptively (see Feature 2's ARTS priority).
- **`lsat/notetypes.py`:** add an `LSAT Structure Probe` notetype (`_SPECS` + `ensure_notetypes`) with fields `stimulus, dimension, category_set, correct_category, skill_tags, source_id, source_quote`. Reuse the `LSAT Item` template's proven pattern (render buttons, `pycmd("lsatAnswer:…")`, capture latency) — I'd factor the click+timing JS so both templates share it.
- **`lsat/events.py`:** log probe answers to the **same** append-only log via `append_event(...)`, adding a `mode` field to `LSAT PerformanceEvent` (`item`/`probe`) so probe RT is analyzable separately but still rides note-sync and the HLC conflict rules. `response_ms` is already first-class.
- **`lsat/ai/` (reuse):** `generator.py` mints probes from source passages and `checker.py` independently verifies the labeled structure with a cited `source_quote` span; `gold_set.py` supplies a human-checked eval set. (Probes are cheap to generate — one stimulus + one label — and easy to check.)
- **`qt/aqt/` + `ts/`:** a Sprint screen — either a new `qt/aqt/lsat_sprint.py` served through `mediasrv`, or a SvelteKit route `ts/routes/lsat-sprint/` mirroring `ts/routes/lsat-dashboard/`. No Rust required for v1.

**(f) Measurable hypothesis + eval design.** _Hypothesis:_ an equal-wall-clock Structure-Sprint arm lowers median structural-classification RT and raises classification accuracy (a within-session learning curve), and **transfers**: higher held-out `P(correct)` on unseen full LR items and lower per-item RT, vs. a no-sprint control. _Eval (reuses `eval/`):_ add a `+sprint` arm to `eval/ablation.py`'s equal-study-time, paired-learner design; **primary metric = held-out P(correct) on unseen LR items (transfer)** with the existing `bootstrap_ci` 95% interval (as the current 3-arm ablation already reports). Add `eval/plm.py` to emit RT/accuracy learning curves and a **pre/post speeded-classification block** (median RT + accuracy), mirroring Kellman & Kaiser's 9-block speed-and-accuracy design. Gate calibration stays green via `eval/calibration.py` (`ECE_MAX`). _Honest caveat, stated in the harness tradition:_ the current learners are **synthetic** (documented in `eval/ablation.py`); this is a pre-registered design + a real A/B once students exist, and the transfer bonus must be an explicit, ablatable parameter, not baked in.

**(g) Cost/risk: 3/5.** New notetype = a schema change (mitigated by `ensure_notetypes` idempotency), a new reviewer surface, and probe content at volume. Dependencies: AI generator/checker throughput; a shared click-latency JS helper. No Rust, no proto changes for v1 — the biggest risk is content validity (bad labels), guarded by the independent checker + `card_check` gate.

### Feature 2 — Fluency Gates (automaticity-aware mastery for the points-at-stake queue)

**(a) Student-facing.** A topic stops being pushed at you only when you are **both accurate and fast** — not just accurate. Each skill shows a three-state fluency badge on the dashboard: **Not yet** (inaccurate) → **Effortful** (accurate but slow, above your personal criterion time) → **Automatic** (accurate _and_ under criterion). "Effortful" topics keep surfacing in the study queue even when your accuracy already looks fine, because slow-but-right is the LSAT failure mode that runs you out of time. You can see your median time per type trending down as a topic goes automatic.

**(b) Mechanism + citations.** Response time is the observable index of learning strength / automatization (Logan, 1988; Shiffrin & Schneider, 1977). ARTS operationalizes this: spacing, sequencing, **and mastery/retirement** are driven by each learner's accuracy _and_ RT, and this **beats fixed expanding/equal spacing** at immediate and delayed tests, with yoked controls proving the benefit is real-time adaptation, not just average spacing (Mettler, Massey & Kellman, 2016). Dual accuracy+speed mastery criteria — "accurate responses to spaced exemplars under a criterion RT" — are the ARTS definition of learned (Mettler, Massey & Kellman, 2014). Kellman (2013) states the design principle directly: _fluency, not just accuracy, is both a means and a goal of learning_ ("no slow, good surgeons").

**(c) Novelty.** _vs. the current app:_ FSRS and the points-at-stake queue use recall/accuracy only; `fold_recent_performance` ignores `response_ms`; the performance model uses RT merely to _predict correctness_, never to declare mastery or reprioritize. Making automaticity a first-class **mastery + scheduling** signal is new here. _vs. the market:_ LSAT Demon _displays_ per-sub-type timing and flags slow types (the closest prior art, and I'd concede it in debate) and 7Sage shows analytics — but neither, on the available evidence, **gates spaced-repetition mastery/retirement on a dual accuracy+speed criterion** that changes what you're served next. The novelty is scheduler-level, not dashboard-level.

**(d) LSAT-specificity.** The exam is severely speeded (35 min ÷ ~25 LR items ≈ 84 s each, twice). "I know how to do these, I just run out of time" is the canonical plateau between mid- and high-160s. A fluency criterion targets precisely that, and the criterion RT can be **exam-calibrated** (e.g. a per-type budget derived from a 35-minute section).

**(e) Implementation sketch (this fork).**

- **`lsat/events.py`:** add `fold_recent_fluency(...)` beside `fold_recent_performance`, returning a per-node `fluency_mastery ∈ [0,1]` that blends recency-weighted accuracy with a speed term — e.g. the recency-weighted fraction of _correct_ recent answers whose `response_ms` is under a per-topic criterion, or a logistic on z-scored log-RT. Reuse the existing half-life recency weighting.
- **`lsat-taxonomy.yaml` + `lsat/taxonomy.py`:** add per-topic `criterion_rt_ms` (target automaticity time), reusing the "weights live in one YAML" pattern and the `Topic` dataclass.
- **`proto` + `rslib` + `pylib` (small, additive):** the cleanest **v1 needs no Rust** — fold fluency into the single `perf_mastery` scalar that `topic_weights_for_queue` already passes per request, so the `points = weight·(1 − mastery)` engine is unchanged. **v2** exposes fluency explicitly: append a `fluency_mastery` field to the `TopicWeight` proto message and extend the Rust tuple to `(tag, weight, acc_mastery, fluency_mastery)` with `mastery = wr·recall + wa·acc + wf·fluency` in `best_points()`. This mirrors the **already-shipped additive proto change** documented in `lsat/docs/points-at-stake-rust-change.md` (append one field / one method → "merge-difficulty LOW"). FSRS internals are untouched — fluency modulates _LSAT points_ (what to surface), keeping the RPC a pure, read-only query.
- **`qt/aqt/lsat_performance.py` + `ts/routes/lsat-dashboard/`:** render the three-state badge and the per-type median-RT trend (the performance model already computes `timing_z` and medians are one fold away).

**(f) Measurable hypothesis + eval design.** _Hypothesis:_ RT-aware mastery/retirement reaches the **same posttest accuracy with fewer trials / less time** (higher _learning efficiency_ = posttest accuracy ÷ time), and produces better durable transfer, than accuracy-only mastery — replicating Mettler et al. (2016) and the 2023 SDT-retirement efficiency win. _Eval:_ add `eval/fluency.py` and an arm to `eval/ablation.py`: {accuracy-only mastery, RT-aware mastery} at an equal review budget; **primary metrics = held-out transfer `P(correct)` and trials-to-criterion**, reported with `bootstrap_ci`; also report efficiency (accuracy ÷ time). Add a guard test that RT-aware mastery does **not** worsen the performance model's calibration (`eval/calibration.py`, `ECE_MAX`) — a real risk if RT is over-weighted.

**(g) Cost/risk: 2/5 (v1) → 3/5 (v2).** v1 is pure Python and reuses the per-request mastery scalar and existing queue, so it ships without touching Rust/proto. v2 adds one proto field + a few Rust lines (low, additive, per the existing change doc). Dependency: enough _timed_ events per topic — the taxonomy already encodes a `min_timed_items` give-up rule, and RT gaming is bounded by the accuracy co-criterion + an upper timeout (ARTS uses a 30 s cap).

### Feature 3 — Minimal-Pair Contrast Drills (discrimination training with signal-detection / d′ retirement)

**(a) Student-facing.** Rapid A/B (or 2-of-N) trials that pair a _genuine_ exemplar of a category with a **near-miss that differs on exactly one structural dimension**: a necessary vs. a sufficient assumption; a real causal claim vs. a mere correlation; "must be true" vs. "could be true"; strengthen vs. "justify the principle." You pick which one matches the target category; feedback then **highlights the single discriminating span** that made the difference. A confusion pair is only retired when your _sensitivity_ is high — persistent mix-ups (you keep "false-alarming" X for Y) keep that exact contrast in rotation.

**(b) Mechanism + citations.** Discrimination is learned fastest by **contrast**: interleaving/juxtaposing category members surfaces the diagnostic difference and improves induction and transfer to novel cases (Kornell & Bjork, 2008; Gibson's differentiation, via Kellman & Garrigan, 2009), and paired-comparison trials are a standard PLM technique (Mettler, Massey & Kellman, 2014; the "comparison training" arm in Cognitive Research, 2023). The retirement rule is the 2023 innovation: use a running **d′** that incorporates **false-alarm rate**, and treat a false alarm as an error that forces the confused category to recur — this reached mastery **more efficiently** than accuracy-based retirement (Kellman, Massey, Krasne & Mettler, 2023). d′ is the right metric because LSAT wrong answers are engineered false alarms.

**(c) Novelty.** _vs. the current app:_ today's interleaving mixes _whole question types_ in study order (`lsat/interleaving.py`) but never constructs **minimal structural pairs**, never records _which_ wrong category was chosen, and can't compute d′. _vs. the market:_ PowerScore/Kaplan teach wrong-answer "families" declaratively and Demon serves "harder" questions, but none run **speeded minimal-pair discrimination with signal-detection mastery**. This is a genuinely new drill type for LSAT.

**(d) LSAT-specificity.** LSAT distractors _are_ minimal pairs by design — sufficient-vs-necessary, must-be-true-vs-most-supported, strengthen-vs-justify. Training the specific discriminations that traps exploit is maximally on-target, and the discriminating-span feedback reuses the existing cited-source-span machinery.

**(e) Implementation sketch (this fork).**

- **`lsat/ai/` (reuse + extend):** `generator.py` produces a correct exemplar plus a **controlled near-miss** that differs only on a named `dimension`, with the discriminating `source_quote` span annotated; `checker.py` independently verifies the pair truly differs on that dimension and nowhere else (the key content-validity guard); `gold_set.py` for eval.
- **`lsat/notetypes.py`:** reuse `LSAT Item`s linked by a `contrast_group` tag, or add a compact `LSAT Contrast` notetype (two stimuli + `dimension` + discriminating span).
- **`lsat/events.py`:** record the **chosen** category, not just correct/incorrect — add a `chosen_category` field to `LSAT PerformanceEvent` — so hits/false-alarms/d′ are recoverable per category while preserving append-only + HLC sync.
- **`lsat/contrast.py` (new):** build contrast sets, compute running per-category d′, and implement **SDT retirement** (retire when d′ ≥ threshold _and_ under criterion RT; a false-alarmed category recurs ~2–3 trials later — the exact 2023 ARTS-SDT parameterization). This d′-mastery feeds the same `perf_mastery`/fluency scalar as Feature 2, so it plugs into the points-at-stake queue with no Rust change.
- **`eval/`:** `eval/contrast.py` reports held-out **d′, hit/false-alarm rates**, and transfer; reuse `eval/leakage.py` (`LEAK_COSINE_MAX`) to ensure generated near-misses don't leak into the held-out set, and `card_check` to gate pair validity.

**(f) Measurable hypothesis + eval design.** _Hypothesis:_ minimal-pair contrast training raises held-out **discrimination sensitivity (d′)** on unseen trap pairs and transfers to full-item accuracy on the _targeted_ confusions, and **SDT (d′) retirement reaches criterion more efficiently than accuracy retirement** (replicating 2023). _Eval:_ three arms — {no-contrast, contrast + accuracy-retirement, contrast + SDT-retirement} — with **primary metrics = held-out d′ and P(correct) on the confusable types**, `bootstrap_ci` intervals, plus the `leakage` and `card_check` gates. Report the fluency-illusion check: learners may rate contrast drills as unpleasant/ineffective even as d′ rises (Kornell & Bjork).

**(g) Cost/risk: 4/5.** Highest content risk — generating _valid_ minimal pairs (a subtly-wrong "near-miss" that is genuinely near and genuinely wrong) is hard; mitigated by the independent checker + `card_check`/`leakage` gates, but this is the feature most likely to ship late. Also needs a new event field (`chosen_category`) and a new drill surface. Dependencies: AI generator/checker quality; Features 2's fluency scalar for retirement.

## Boldest bet

**Ship "Structure Sprints" — the missing PLM primitive.** Add a seconds-per-trial, RT-scored _structural-classification_ drill (question-type / conclusion-location / flaw-family), because the biggest, most durable cross-domain PL effects come from exactly this task and it attacks the single trait that separates elite scorers: fast, automatic recognition of argument structure — the one dimension this fork already measures (`response_ms`) but has never trained.

## Threats to validity (what a debater should attack, and my answers)

- **"The eval is synthetic."** True today — `eval/ablation.py` uses documented synthetic learners and an explicit transfer-bonus parameter. My features ship with _pre-registered_ designs and ablatable parameters; the honest claim is "the methodology is sound and the effect is large in adjacent domains," not "proven on LSAT students yet."
- **"Perceptual training won't reach expert level."** Conceded (Cognitive Research, 2023). PLMs are a fluency accelerator on top of FSRS + content, targeting the _speed_ plateau, not a replacement for reasoning instruction.
- **"LSAT Demon already tracks timing."** Yes — it _reports_ per-type timing. None of the tools _train_ a speeded classification micro-task (F1), _gate scheduling_ on a dual accuracy+speed criterion (F2), or use _d′_ discrimination mastery (F3). The novelty is in the trainer and the scheduler, not the dashboard.
- **"RT can be gamed / is noisy."** Bound speed with an accuracy co-criterion and an upper timeout (ARTS uses 30 s), z-score within learner, and require spaced reps — all standard ARTS safeguards.
