# Speedrun — Research Support for Proposed Features

This document backs the features in `Speedrun_AI_Features.md` with the learning-science and LSAT-domain evidence they rest on. It is organized around the two design theses (the Spiky Points of View), because each feature exists to serve one of them. A short **What this does not establish** section follows each thesis, in keeping with the project's rule that honest uncertainty scores higher than a confident claim you cannot back.

Citations name real, well-known works; exact volume and page numbers should be verified against the originals before this text goes into a formal Brainlift.

**July 2026 augmentation.** This revision adds effect sizes, exact sources, and three research-forced design changes (marked **Design change** below), and cross-references the deeper cited corpus in [`research/`](research/README.md) (four research memos + the multi-agent debate record in `research/debate/DECISION.md`). The three findings that changed _features_, not just prose:

1. Corrected confident errors **return** unless re-tested — so B3 gains a **mandatory spaced re-test**, and B2 tracks whether the fix stuck (Butler, Fazio & Marsh 2011; Metcalfe & Miele 2014).
2. Interleaving pays most for **confusable** categories — so A2 interleaves confusable question-type clusters, not a uniform shuffle (Brunmair & Richter 2019, g = 0.42 overall, larger with between-category similarity).
3. In-app feedback rarely **trains** calibration — so D1/D2 measure miscalibration and discount readiness with it; the app never claims to coach it away (Gruetzemacher et al.; Bongard et al.).

## Thesis 1 — The LSAT is a knowledge test wearing a reasoning test's costume

**The design claim.** For the LSAT, the memorizable knowledge base is a set of _reasoning primitives_ — precise diction, formal-logic rules and named fallacies, and the question-type taxonomy with its prescribed approach. Building the memory layer out of these (Features A1–A3) is what makes a flashcard-based score model viable for an exam the spec itself calls the hard case because "there are almost no facts to memorize."

**Reasoning is domain-specific (supports A1, A3).** Willingham argues that the "critical thinking skills" people hope to teach in the abstract are in practice bound to domain knowledge: people reason well about material they know and structures they recognize, and poorly otherwise (Willingham, "Critical Thinking: Why Is It So Hard to Teach?", _American Educator_, 2007; expanded in _Why Don't Students Like School?_, 2009). The LSAT markets itself as requiring no domain knowledge, but recurring flaw types, assumption patterns, and a fixed question taxonomy _are_ a domain. Making them the explicit content of the deck is the operational form of Willingham's finding.

**Cognitive load explains why diction is a memory target (supports A1).** Cognitive Load Theory holds that working memory is sharply limited and that capacity spent decoding unfamiliar terms or connectives is capacity unavailable for the reasoning the item is actually testing (Sweller, "Cognitive Load During Problem Solving," _Cognitive Science_, 1988; Sweller, van Merriënboer & Paas, 1998). If a candidate must stop to work out _unless_, _only if_, or a qualifier mid-passage, load rises and performance falls. Automating recall of precise diction through spaced retrieval frees that capacity — which is why `diction/*` cards belong in the memory layer rather than being treated as "not really facts."

**Identification is the first skill, and mixing types helps (supports A2, and the decision to demonstrate A2 via the paraphrase gap).** Weinstein and Sumeracki synthesize the evidence that interleaving related problem types improves the ability to _discriminate_ between them and select the right approach, whereas blocking one type inflates in-session performance without building that discrimination (Weinstein, Madan & Sumeracki, "Teaching the Science of Learning," _Cognitive Research: Principles and Implications_, 2018; Weinstein, Sumeracki & Caviglioli, _Understanding How We Learn_, Routledge, 2018). Interleaving's benefit for category discrimination is well documented in the mathematics literature (Rohrer & Taylor, "The Shuffling of Mathematics Problems Improves Learning," _Instructional Science_, 2007; Rohrer, Dedrick & Stershic, 2015). The direct implication for A2 is a card that asks _what type / what flaw is this_ before asking for the solution, and a review order that mixes types rather than blocking them.

**How big, and when — the interleaving effect is moderated by confusability (sharpens A2).** The multilevel meta-analysis of the interleaving effect (59 studies, 238 effect sizes) finds a moderate overall benefit, **Hedges' g = 0.42** [0.34, 0.50], with a decisive moderator pattern: the effect _grows with between-category similarity_ and with material complexity, and can even reverse for easily-separated material (blocking beat interleaving for word learning, g = −0.39) (Brunmair & Richter, "Similarity Matters: A Meta-Analysis of Interleaved Learning and Its Moderators," _Psychological Bulletin_, 2019, [doi:10.1037/bul0000209](https://doi.org/10.1037/bul0000209)). The proposed mechanism is discriminative contrast — juxtaposing members of _confusable_ categories highlights the features that separate them (Rohrer, "Interleaving Helps Students Distinguish Among Similar Concepts," _Educational Psychology Review_, 2012; Birnbaum, Kornell, Bjork & Bjork, "Why Interleaving Enhances Inductive Learning," _Memory & Cognition_, 2013; Kang & Pashler 2012).

> **Design change (A2).** Uniformly shuffling all question types is the weak version of this evidence. The strong version interleaves the types students actually _confuse_ — the stem families whose cues overlap (Necessary vs. Sufficient Assumption vs. Strengthen vs. Principle; Must Be True vs. Most Strongly Supported; Flaw vs. Method vs. Parallel). A2's review order therefore mixes within these **confusable clusters** so each item demands a genuine discrimination, which is exactly the identification skill the classify stage grades.

**A fixed, attackable taxonomy exists in the domain (supports A1, A3, and B4's source grounding).** Practitioner expertise converges on sorting LR items into a fixed set — Must Be True, Weaken, Strengthen, Assumption, Flaw, Parallel Reasoning, Resolve the Paradox, and so on — with a prescribed approach per type (the PowerScore method associated with Dave Killoran; 7Sage's per-question analysis associated with J.Y. Ping). This taxonomy is what A1 encodes as `qtype/*`, what A3 measures coverage against, and the documented catalog that B4's misconception labels must trace back to.

**What this does not establish.** The evidence supports that these primitives are _necessary_ and that automating their recall reduces load and aids discrimination. It does **not** show they are _sufficient_ — knowing a flaw's definition is not the same as spotting it in a novel argument under time. That gap is precisely why the memory and performance layers are kept separate (C1) and why the paraphrase test (C2, spec §7d) is the honest check on whether the bridge was actually built. If reworded-item accuracy tracks raw recall, the performance model is echoing memory and Thesis 1 has been over-applied.

## Thesis 2 — The misses worth deep review are the ones you were confident about

**The design claim.** Capturing confidence _before_ the answer is revealed (B1), classifying each review by confidence × correctness (B2), and prioritizing confident-wrong items in the scheduler (B3) targets misconceptions — the errors most likely to recur — rather than treating all wrong answers alike. Miscalibration then informs how much to trust the readiness number (D1, D2).

**The hypercorrection effect is the direct empirical support (supports B1, B2, B3).** Errors made with _high_ confidence are more likely to be corrected after feedback than errors made with low confidence — the hypercorrection effect (Butterfield & Metcalfe, "Errors Committed with High Confidence Are Hypercorrected," _Journal of Experimental Psychology: Learning, Memory, and Cognition_, 2001; reviewed in Metcalfe, "Learning from Errors," _Annual Review of Psychology_, 2017). A confident wrong answer reflects a firmly held but incorrect belief; surfacing and correcting it yields outsized learning, and it also flags a misconception likely to reappear on similar items. This is the mechanism behind ranking confident-wrong cards ahead of others in B3, and behind routing unsure-wrong items to relearning rather than the same high-priority path.

**The correction does not stick by itself — confident errors return, and re-testing blocks the return (forces the B3 re-test; supports B2's "did the fix stick" tracking).** Butler, Fazio & Marsh followed hypercorrection out to a one-week delay: the effect persisted but error correction _decreased_, and when subjects failed to correct an error at the delay they tended to reproduce their original answer — with **high-confidence errors more likely than low-confidence errors to be reproduced** ("The Hypercorrection Effect Persists Over a Week, but High-Confidence Errors Return," _Psychonomic Bulletin & Review_, 2011, [doi:10.3758/s13423-011-0173-y](https://doi.org/10.3758/s13423-011-0173-y)). The remedy is also demonstrated: interposing a _retrieval test_ after corrective feedback "not only greatly enhanced memory for the correct answers but also **blocked the return of the errors**" (Metcalfe & Miele, "Hypercorrection of High Confidence Errors: Prior Testing Both Enhances Delayed Performance and Blocks the Return of the Errors," _Journal of Applied Research in Memory and Cognition_, 3(3), 189–197, 2014, [doi:10.1016/j.jarmac.2014.04.001](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jarmac.2014.04.001)).

> **Design change (B2/B3).** Surfacing a confident-wrong card once and showing feedback is the _incomplete_ intervention — the misconception predictably re-emerges as the correction fades. B3 therefore schedules a **mandatory spaced re-test** of every misconception-state item (a fresh graded retrieval, not a re-read), and B2 tracks each misconception's **resolution status** (did the later re-test succeed?) so a "corrected" misconception that relapses is re-flagged rather than silently trusted.

**Confidence must be measured, not inferred — hence a pre-answer field (supports B1).** The hypercorrection literature relies on a confidence judgment made about a _specific response_, distinct from a retrospective sense of how hard the item felt. Anki's Again/Hard/Good/Easy is collected after the answer is shown and conflates difficulty with ease of future recall; it is not a pre-answer confidence-in-this-response signal. Capturing that signal therefore requires the new field and pre-reveal UI in B1, not a reinterpretation of the existing buttons.

**This automates blind review, a mainstream LSAT method (supports B1–B3 and the product framing; practitioner evidence, not an RCT).** Blind review — flag every question you are not certain about during the timed run, re-answer the flagged ones untimed before checking, then study hardest the ones you were confident about yet missed — is the dominant contemporary LSAT preparation method, associated with J.Y. Ping and 7Sage. Its vocabulary maps one-to-one onto B2's states: 7Sage explicitly names the **"Confidence Error"** — a miss on an _uncircled_ (confident) question — as the most dangerous case, "pre-confusion... danger for which you don't even have a radar," usually caused by an engineered trap answer, and prescribes exactly the trap-autopsy review B3 prioritizes (7Sage, "The Blind Review: How to Correctly Prep for LSAT," parts 2 and 5, [7sage.com/blog](https://7sage.com/blog/the-blind-review-how-to-correctly-prep-for-lsat-part-2)). The timed-vs-blind-review score gap is likewise the community's standard diagnostic for _speed problem vs. fundamentals problem_. B1–B3 automate and scale this practice rather than inventing a mechanic — a defensible claim for the Brainlift, provided it is labeled what it is: converging practitioner wisdom, not controlled evidence.

**Practice testing and error analysis are high-utility, and drive learning (supports the whole confidence-and-error loop).** Dunlosky and colleagues' large review rated practice testing and distributed practice as the two highest-utility techniques across conditions (Dunlosky, Rawson, Marsh, Nathan & Willingham, "Improving Students' Learning With Effective Learning Techniques," _Psychological Science in the Public Interest_, 2013). The testing effect — that retrieval itself strengthens learning, not merely measures it — is well established (Roediger & Karpicke, "Test-Enhanced Learning," _Psychological Science_, 2006). Orienting the app around graded retrieval, and around analyzing the errors that retrieval exposes, sits on this base.

**What this does not establish.** The hypercorrection effect is demonstrated for correcting known errors after feedback; the further step — that a _scheduler_ prioritizing confident-wrong cards raises accuracy on _new, related_ questions at equal study time — is a plausible extension but not something the cited studies prove. That is exactly why B3 is the pre-registered ablation (spec §8): the app runs a fair test that could show the feature makes no difference, and reports that outcome if it occurs. Two further boundaries: the hypercorrection literature uses general-knowledge _recall_ items, not multi-step reasoning under time pressure, so the size of the effect on LSAT items is an open question the ablation addresses rather than assumes; and self-reported confidence is noisy for some students (e.g., those who tap through without reading) — D1's calibration chart is partly a diagnostic for that.

## Supporting Thesis — Honest readiness requires calibration, not a single number

**The design claim.** The readiness display (D3) must show a range, coverage, and a "how sure" indicator, and must abstain when data is thin; miscalibration widens the range (D2). This is the spec's honesty rule made concrete.

**Calibration is measurable and belongs in the readiness story (supports D1, D2, D3, and spec §Step 1).** A well-calibrated predictor's 80% claims come true about 80% of the time, and calibration is scorable with proper scoring rules such as the Brier score and log loss (Brier, "Verification of Forecasts Expressed in Terms of Probability," _Monthly Weather Review_, 1950; on the value of proper scoring and probabilistic honesty, Gneiting & Raftery, 2007). Reporting calibration on held-back reviews, and refusing a point estimate when coverage or calibration is inadequate, is the difference the spec draws between a prediction and "a guess in a nice font."

**Desirable difficulties frame why the harder-but-honest path is the right one (supports the overall design stance).** Conditions that slow apparent progress — spacing, interleaving, retrieval, confronting errors — often produce stronger durable learning than conditions that feel fluent (Bjork & Bjork, "Making Things Hard on Yourself, But in a Good Way," 2011). This is the through-line linking A2, B3, and the decision to measure and display gaps rather than smooth them over.

**Calibration is measurable, but not reliably trainable by an app — measure it, discount by it, don't promise to fix it (constrains D1, D2).** Scalable calibration-feedback training has a weak record: two experiments (N = 610 and 871) comparing outcome feedback and scoring-rule performance feedback against a control found **no improvement in calibration from either regime** (Gruetzemacher, Lee & Paradice, "Calibration Feedback With the Practical Scoring Rule Does Not Improve Calibration of Confidence," _Futures & Foresight Science_, [doi:10.1002/ffo2.199](https://doi.org/10.1002/ffo2.199)). Where automated calibration feedback does move judgments, its main demonstrated effect is _lowering confidence_ wholesale rather than sharpening the mapping between confidence and accuracy (Bongard et al., three studies with a personalized calibration-diagram intervention). Intensive facilitated training can help, but that is not what a study app delivers. The design consequence: D1 shows the student their reliability curve as a _diagnostic_ ("your 'confident' answers are right 68% of the time"), and D2 uses measured miscalibration to _widen the readiness range_ — the app never claims the chart itself will calibrate the student, and any improvement claim would need its own test.

> **Design change (D1/D2).** Earlier framing treated the calibration chart as implicitly corrective. The evidence supports measurement, not treatment: calibration feeds the _honesty machinery_ (range widening, abstention) rather than the coaching copy.

**Transfer must be reported as a graded curve, not a single number (sharpens C2).** Transfer varies along measurable dimensions of distance between the learning and testing situations, and single far-transfer effect sizes are considered misleading (Barnett & Ceci, "When and Where Do We Apply What We Learn? A Taxonomy for Far Transfer," _Psychological Bulletin_, 2002). C2 therefore reports the recall-vs-reworded gap _per topic_ and, where surface distance can be computed, as a distance-graded curve — never one headline number.

**What this does not establish.** A genuine score model needs students who study and then take real practice tests, tracked over time — data not honestly obtainable in a week (spec §9). Nothing here licenses a validated 120–180 projection. The defensible position, and the one the spec rewards, is to grade the steps of the bridge (calibrated memory, held-out performance prediction, a documented mapping with a range) and to state plainly where real-student validation is still missing.
